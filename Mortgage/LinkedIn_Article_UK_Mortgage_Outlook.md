## First, Let Me Explain How We Got Here

If you've felt financially squeezed these past few years, you're not imagining it. The data shows exactly what happened.

Between December 2021 and August 2023, the Bank of England raised interest rates **14 times in a row** ‚Äì taking them from basically zero (0.1%) all the way up to 5.25%. This was the fastest rate rise in modern UK history, and it was designed to tackle inflation that had shot above 11%.

**Here's what that meant in real terms:**

- In early 2021, a typical 2-year fixed mortgage (75% loan-to-value) was around 1.5%
- By late 2023, that same mortgage peaked above 6%
- If you had a ¬£250,000 mortgage, your monthly payments could have nearly doubled

I remember the sense of panic every time another rate rise was announced. Month after month, it just kept going up.

**Here's the timeline of what happened:**
- **3 August 2023**: Rates hit their peak at 5.25%
- **1 August 2024**: First cut ‚Äì down to 5.00%

## Here's Something Important: Mortgage Rates Don't Fall as Fast as Base Rates

When I first started this analysis, I assumed that if the base rate drops 0.5%, then mortgage rates would drop 0.5% too. Turns out, that's not how it works.

Here's why:

**Banks need to make money** ‚Äì They maintain their profit margins (lending spreads) to cover costs and risks. They're not going to pass on the full rate cut to you.

**Market expectations matter** ‚Äì Longer-term mortgage rates don't just reflect today's base rate. They're also influenced by what the market thinks rates will be in the future.

**Different products respond differently** ‚Äì Short-term fixes move more with base rate changes. Long-term fixes are less sensitive.

Here's roughly how it breaks down:
- **2-year fixed rates** respond to about 70% of base rate changes (most sensitive)
- **5-year fixed rates** respond to about 50% of changes (moderate)
- **10-year fixed rates** respond to about 30% of changes (least sensitive)

So if the base rate drops 0.5 percentage points, your 2-year fixed might drop around 0.35pp, but your 10-year fixed might only drop 0.15pp.
